Image filtering

Image filtering is a fundamental technique in image processing and computer vision used to enhance or modify images by applying various mathematical operations to each pixel or neighborhood of pixels. These operations can include smoothing, sharpening, edge detection, noise reduction, and more. Image filtering plays a crucial role in tasks such as feature extraction, image enhancement, object recognition, and noise reduction.

Uses of filtering:

  • Enhance an image (denoise, resize, increase contrast, etc)
  • Extract information (texture, edges, interest points, etc)
  • Detect patterns (template matching)

Types of filtering:

  • Linear Filters: Linear filters apply a linear transformation to the pixel values in the image or a neighborhood of pixels. Common linear filters include:
    • Smoothing Filters: Reduce image noise and detail by averaging pixel values within a local region. Gaussian blur and mean filter are examples of smoothing filters.
    • Sharpening Filters Enhance image details and edges by accentuating high-frequency components. Examples include the Laplacian filter and the Sobel operator.
    • Edge Detection Filters: highlight edges and boundaries in images by detecting abrupt changes in pixel intensity.
  • Non-linear Filters: Non-linear filters apply operations that are not linear combinations of pixel values. They are often used for tasks such as noise reduction and image enhancement. Examples include:
    • Median Filter: Replaces each pixel value with the median value of its neighborhood, effectively reducing impulse noise (such as salt-and-pepper noise).
    • Bilateral Filter: Smooths images while preserving edges by considering both spatial and intensity differences between pixels.
    • Anisotropic Diffusion: Diffusion-based filtering method that selectively smooths image regions while preserving strong edges.

Image Formation using Lenses

Lenses are optical components designed to focus light onto a sensor or film, altering the path of incoming light rays through refraction. They come in various shapes and designs, each with specific properties that affect image formation, such as focal length, aperture size, and lens aberrations.

When light rays pass through a lens, they converge or diverge based on the lens's curvature and refractive properties. Unlike the pinhole model, which relies on a single point of light passing through a tiny aperture, lenses can gather light from a broader area, allowing for brighter and sharper images.

The process of image formation with lenses involves several key elements:

  • Focal Length: The distance between the lens and its focal point, where parallel rays of light converge after passing through the lens. Focal length determines the magnification and field of view of the resulting image.
  • Aperture: The opening in the lens that controls the amount of light entering the camera. Adjusting the aperture size regulates the depth of field and influences the overall brightness and sharpness of the image.
  • Lens Aberrations: Imperfections in lens design that can distort or degrade image quality. Common aberrations include spherical aberration, chromatic aberration, and distortion, which can affect the clarity, color accuracy, and geometry of the captured image.

Digital Images

A digital image is an array of numbers indicating the image irradiance at various points on the image plane

Image intensities are spatially sampled. This means that we sample the 2D space on a regular grid

ntensity values are quantized (8-bits, 10-bits, 12-bits), meaning we quantize each sample (round to nearest integer)

Thus, images can be represented as a matrix of int values
