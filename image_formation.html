Image Formation

What are images?

Images are a projection of 3D scene onto 2D plane.

We need to understand the geometric and photometric relation between the scene and its image.

To do so we should understand:

  • Pinhole and Perspective Projection
  • Image formation using Lenses

Pinhole and Perspective Projection

Pinhole Projection

The pinhole projection model is one of the simplest yet most crucial concepts in understanding how images are formed in cameras. At its core, it involves light passing through a small aperture (the "pinhole") and projecting an inverted image onto a surface placed behind it. This model operates on the principle of light rays traveling in straight lines. Each point in the scene being captured emits light rays in all directions, but only those that pass through the pinhole and hit the imaging plane create the final image.

Perspective Projection

Perspective projection builds upon the pinhole model, incorporating additional complexities such as the notion of perspective and depth. In perspective projection, objects that are farther away from the camera appear smaller in the image compared to objects that are closer. This effect mimics how our eyes perceive depth in the real world, creating a sense of three-dimensionality in the captured image.

How They Are Used

Pinhole and perspective projection are used extensively in various fields, including computer graphics, computer vision, photography, and cinematography. In computer vision, these concepts serve as the basis for understanding how cameras capture images of the world and how these images can be processed and analyzed by algorithms.

Computer vision applications often rely on camera calibration, which involves determining the intrinsic parameters of a camera (such as focal length, principal point, and lens distortion) to accurately interpret images and extract meaningful information from them. Pinhole and perspective projection models play a crucial role in this calibration process, allowing computer vision systems to understand how objects in a scene are projected onto the camera sensor.

Understanding pinhole and perspective projection is essential for several reasons:

  • Camera Calibration: Proper calibration enables accurate measurements and reconstructions of objects in a scene, which is vital for applications such as 3D reconstruction, object tracking, and augmented reality.
  • Depth Perception: Perspective projection provides depth cues in images, allowing computer vision algorithms to estimate the distance to objects in a scene. This depth information is crucial for tasks like scene understanding, obstacle avoidance, and robotic navigation
  • Image Formation: Knowledge of how images are formed helps in designing and optimizing camera systems for specific applications, such as surveillance, autonomous vehicles, medical imaging, and industrial inspection.

Limitatioins

Pinholes pass less light and hence require long exposures to capture bright images

Image Formation using Lenses

Lenses are optical components designed to focus light onto a sensor or film, altering the path of incoming light rays through refraction. They come in various shapes and designs, each with specific properties that affect image formation, such as focal length, aperture size, and lens aberrations.

When light rays pass through a lens, they converge or diverge based on the lens's curvature and refractive properties. Unlike the pinhole model, which relies on a single point of light passing through a tiny aperture, lenses can gather light from a broader area, allowing for brighter and sharper images.

The process of image formation with lenses involves several key elements:

  • Focal Length: The distance between the lens and its focal point, where parallel rays of light converge after passing through the lens. Focal length determines the magnification and field of view of the resulting image.
  • Aperture: The opening in the lens that controls the amount of light entering the camera. Adjusting the aperture size regulates the depth of field and influences the overall brightness and sharpness of the image.
  • Lens Aberrations: Imperfections in lens design that can distort or degrade image quality. Common aberrations include spherical aberration, chromatic aberration, and distortion, which can affect the clarity, color accuracy, and geometry of the captured image.

Digital Images

A digital image is an array of numbers indicating the image irradiance at various points on the image plane

Image intensities are spatially sampled. This means that we sample the 2D space on a regular grid

ntensity values are quantized (8-bits, 10-bits, 12-bits), meaning we quantize each sample (round to nearest integer)
